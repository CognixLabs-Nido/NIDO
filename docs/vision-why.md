# Why I'm building NIDO

## "Iker, ¿dónde está?"

I spent a couple of days during the holidays visiting the kids at my mother's nursery school. I went there to play with them. The next day, when I didn't show up, they asked for me — _"Iker, ¿dónde está?"_ ("Iker, where is he?"). My mother told me when I got home. It made me happier than I expected, and it made me understand something that stayed with me: this isn't an abstract thesis about technology in early childhood education. It's these people, this place, these kids.

## The problem

My mother runs ANAIA, an early childhood school (ages 0-3) in Valencia. She had spent months looking for a better agenda system for the centre. Three years ago she had moved from paper to digital, and even the digital tool wasn't enough — it was missing things, it had unnecessary ones, the tool that was supposed to save her time was actually costing her time. Talking with her at home, one thing became clear quickly: it wasn't only ANAIA. My mother is in a WhatsApp group of over 100 directors of other early childhood schools in Valencia. Each one had tried two or three different solutions. None of them was completely happy with what she was using. They were all paying for a tool that felt half-finished. That's the problem I saw.

## The click

I'm finishing my GCSEs. In Computer Science I've built applications — small ones, still, but I've learned that going from an idea to something that works is something I can do. When I started to understand my mother's problem and her colleagues' problem in detail, the thought was direct: _I can build this_. It's not arrogance. It's realising I have the basic pieces in place: I can code, the problem is validated by more than one person, and I have direct and honest access to someone who lives it every day. It's not common to find those three things at once.

## What I did next

I talked to my father, to test the idea with someone with a business mind. A few weeks earlier another opportunity had come up: a project with friends to build VERTEX Performance, a sports management company with a _tecnificación_ agreement with a club in Valencia, which includes a platform for athletic development, training, physical conditioning and team management. We decided to do it properly — to form a limited company so we'd have structure from the start and be able to scale later. I'm 16 and I'm the CTO of VERTEX. My concrete role: build the website, build the app, manage social media and the advertising budget. Learning while doing.

NIDO fits into this context. It's not an isolated project. It's part of a way of starting to work that I plan to sustain over time — building products for problems I know well, with a plan and a long-term view. What does this imply? The hypothesis is simple: if NIDO works in ANAIA and in one or two more centres from my mother's network, there is a scalable business across the 0-3 early childhood schools of Valencia first, and the rest of Spain after.

## What it means to me if NIDO works

Two things, in this order.

For my mother and the directors: that they get their time back, that the management tool stops being a daily friction and becomes a real ally, that communication with families is clean, that documenting the day-to-day doesn't drain the life out of them, that they can go home earlier and in better shape.

For me: to prove to myself — and prove to the world — that I can create something that solves a real need, that this thing I'm passionate about has practical use in the market, not just as a hobby, that I can dedicate myself professionally to building things that matter, and to get better every day by learning from the process. There's also a question I'm asking myself deliberately with this project: can I take something from zero to launch with everything it involves — research, technical decisions, hard conversations, mistakes, iteration, putting it in the hands of real users and standing up to the feedback? NIDO is where I'm going to find out.

## Where I'm going

I start A-levels this September. I'll apply to universities in 2026/2027 for entry in September 2028. I'm looking at programmes in the United States, the United Kingdom and Spain. What I'm looking for in a university isn't prestige for the sake of prestige. I'm looking for people smarter than me, an environment of people who want to change things, and professors who have built things in the real world and not only thought about them. NIDO is part of the path, not the destination. The destination is to keep building, so that my thirty-year-old self is a massively better version of my sixteen-year-old self, with the same drive and a lot more learning behind me. And so that when I visit the kids at ANAIA, they still ask for me.

_Iker — Valencia, 2026_
